Cardiovascular System

Overview

The cardiovascular system transports oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and wastes while maintaining homeostasis. Blood acts as the transport medium, and the heart functions as the pump.

Blood

Blood functions include transport, regulation, and protection. Blood is composed of plasma (55%) and formed elements (45%). Plasma contains water, proteins, electrolytes, nutrients, hormones, and wastes. Formed elements include erythrocytes, leukocytes, and platelets.

Platelets form from megakaryocytes in red bone marrow. Hemostasis prevents blood loss through vascular spasm, platelet plug formation, and coagulation.

Blood type compatibility is determined by A, B, and Rh antigens. Compatibility prevents agglutination and hemolysis.

Heart Anatomy and Circulation

The heart has four chambers: right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, and left ventricle. Blood enters through the venae cavae and pulmonary veins and exits via the pulmonary trunk and aorta.

Pulmonary circulation moves blood to the lungs, while systemic circulation delivers blood to the rest of the body. CO2 + H2O = H2CO3 = H+ + HCO3-

Blood flow pathway:

Vena cava → Right atrium → Tricuspid valve → Right ventricle → Pulmonary valve → Pulmonary arteries → Lungs → Pulmonary veins → Left atrium → Mitral valve → Left ventricle → Aortic valve → Aorta

Venous return is supported by the skeletal muscle pump, respiratory pump, and venous valves.

Cardiac Physiology

Cardiac output is calculated by multiplying heart rate by stroke volume. Mean arterial pressure depends on cardiac output and peripheral resistance.

Electrical Conduction

Electrical conduction follows the SA node, AV node, bundle of His, bundle branches, and Purkinje fibers.

On an ECG, the P wave represents atrial depolarization, the QRS complex represents ventricular depolarization, and the T wave represents ventricular repolarization.

Reflection

Understanding blood and heart physiology improves clinical reasoning and supports safe and effective patient care.
