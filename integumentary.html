Integumentary System

Overview

The integumentary system includes the skin and its accessory structures. It forms the body’s outer covering and plays a major role in protection, sensation, temperature regulation, and maintaining homeostasis.

Major components:

1. Skin
2. Hair and nails
3. Cutaneous glands such as sweat and sebaceous glands

Primary functions:

1. Protection from pathogens, injury, and water loss
2. Thermoregulation through sweating and blood vessel changes
3. Sensation through touch, pressure, pain, and temperature receptors
4. Metabolic functions such as vitamin D synthesis

Histology (Microscopic Structure)

The integumentary system is made of several layers and accessory structures, and each one has specific cell types, microscopic features, and functions.

Structure Cell Types Present Key Features Function
Epidermis Keratinocytes, melanocytes, Langerhans cells, Merkel cells Keratinized stratified squamous epithelium, avascular, arranged in layers from stratum basale to stratum corneum Protection, waterproofing, pathogen barrier, helps reduce water loss
Dermis Fibroblasts, macrophages, mast cells, immune cells, blood vessel cells Made of connective tissue, contains collagen and elastic fibers, blood vessels, nerves, follicles, and glands Strength, elasticity, nourishment of epidermis, sensation, temperature regulation
Hypodermis Adipocytes, fibroblasts, connective tissue cells Mostly areolar and adipose tissue, rich in fat, not technically part of the skin but closely associated with it Insulation, cushioning, energy storage, anchors skin to deeper tissues
Hair Follicles Keratinocytes, melanocytes Tubular invaginations of the epidermis extending into the dermis, often associated with sebaceous glands and arrector pili muscles Hair production, protection, sensation
Nails Keratinocytes Hard keratin plates produced by the nail matrix, grow over the nail bed Protect the distal digits and help with grasping and fine movement
Sweat Glands (eccrine/apocrine) Glandular epithelial cells Eccrine glands are widely distributed and produce watery sweat. Apocrine glands are found mainly in the axillary and groin regions and produce a thicker secretion. Thermoregulation, excretion, scent-related function in apocrine glands
Sebaceous Glands Sebocytes Oil glands usually associated with hair follicles, secrete sebum Lubricate skin and hair and help reduce drying and microbial growth

Important Chemistry / Biochemistry

Main structural protein in epidermis: Keratin

Structural proteins in dermis: Collagen and elastin

Pigments affecting skin color: Melanin, carotene, and hemoglobin

Role of lipids in skin: Lipids help create a waterproof barrier, prevent dehydration, reduce water loss, and provide part of the skin’s chemical defense.

Vitamin D synthesis starts with: 7-dehydrocholesterol
and requires: ultraviolet light from the sun

Clinical Relevance

Barrier loss / Wound healing stages:

Hemostasis → Inflammation → Proliferation → Remodeling

Skin cancers and their cell of origin:

Basal cell carcinoma: cells of the stratum basale
Squamous cell carcinoma: keratinocytes, especially from more superficial epidermal layers
Melanoma: melanocytes

Burn classification:

1st degree: damage to the epidermis only
2nd degree: damage to the epidermis and part of the dermis
3rd degree: damage through the full thickness of the skin and possibly deeper tissues

Clinical Examples

Case Presentation Underlying Cause (Histology/Chemistry) Nursing Considerations
A Elderly patient with a pressure ulcer Prolonged pressure reduces blood flow to the skin and underlying tissue, causing ischemia, tissue breakdown, and delayed wound healing. Frequent repositioning, pressure relief, wound care, skin assessment, and support for nutrition and hydration
B Teen with cystic acne Blocked sebaceous glands, excess sebum production, inflammation, and bacterial involvement lead to deep lesions in hair follicles and glands. Skin hygiene education, medication use as prescribed, monitor for scarring, and support adherence to treatment
C Patient with 3rd-degree burn Full-thickness destruction of epidermis and dermis causes barrier loss, nerve ending damage, and severe fluid loss. Fluid replacement, infection prevention, pain management, temperature regulation, and wound/burn care monitoring
D Patient with severe sunburn after prolonged UV exposure Ultraviolet radiation damages epidermal cells and causes inflammation, redness, and possible blistering. Repeated injury increases mutation risk. Hydration, pain relief, skin protection, patient education about sunscreen and UV exposure, and monitoring for complications

Integration With Other Systems

Skin + immune system: The skin acts as a first line of defense, and immune cells such as Langerhans cells help detect and respond to pathogens.

Skin + nervous system: The skin contains sensory receptors that detect touch, pain, temperature, and pressure, sending information to the nervous system.

Skin + endocrine system: The skin begins vitamin D synthesis, which is later activated by the liver and kidneys and affects calcium balance.

Skin + cardiovascular system: Dermal blood vessels help regulate body temperature through vasodilation and vasoconstriction, and they also supply nutrients to skin tissues.

Key Terms to Define

Keratinization: The process in which epidermal cells fill with keratin, lose their nuclei, and become tough protective cells.

Melanogenesis: The production of melanin by melanocytes.

Desquamation: The shedding of dead cells from the outer layer of the epidermis.

Hyperkeratosis: An abnormal thickening of the outer layer of skin due to increased keratin production.

Photoaging: Premature aging of the skin caused by long-term ultraviolet exposure.

Further Your Understanding

Identify key chemical processes relevant to this system.

Important chemical processes in the integumentary system include keratin production, melanin synthesis, lipid secretion, sweat production, vitamin D synthesis, and inflammatory signaling during wound healing. Ion balance is also important because fluid shifts and electrolyte loss can occur with sweating and burns.

Discuss important molecules and how this system contributes to maintaining internal balance.

Important molecules include oxygen, water, electrolytes, lipids, melanin, keratin, and vitamin D precursors. The skin helps maintain homeostasis by controlling water loss, protecting against infection, regulating temperature, and beginning vitamin D synthesis. One major negative feedback process involves temperature regulation: when body temperature rises, sweat glands become more active and blood vessels dilate to release heat. When body temperature falls, sweating decreases and blood vessels constrict to conserve heat.

Explain how melanin is produced.

Melanin is produced in melanocytes from the amino acid tyrosine through enzyme-controlled reactions involving tyrosinase. The melanin is packaged into melanosomes and transferred to keratinocytes, where it helps protect nuclear DNA from ultraviolet radiation.

Why do burns cause fluid loss? How do we calculate TBSA?

Burns destroy the protective skin barrier, so fluid can leak out of damaged tissues and evaporate more easily. This can cause dehydration and electrolyte imbalance. TBSA means total body surface area, and it is commonly estimated using the rule of nines in adults.

Compare eccrine vs. apocrine sweat glands.

Eccrine sweat glands are found over most of the body and secrete a watery sweat that mainly helps with thermoregulation. Apocrine sweat glands are found mainly in the axillary and groin areas, become active at puberty, and produce a thicker secretion that can develop odor after bacterial breakdown.

Describe how vitamin D is synthesized in the skin.

Ultraviolet light converts 7-dehydrocholesterol in the skin into a vitamin D precursor. This molecule is then modified in the liver and kidneys to form active vitamin D, which helps regulate calcium and phosphorus levels.
