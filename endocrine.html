Body System Page

Endocrine System

Overview

Hormones regulate metabolism, growth, hydration, blood pressure, glucose control, reproduction, and the stress response. Nurses rely on hormone knowledge to interpret lab values, recognize early signs of disease, monitor medication effects, and prevent complications such as dehydration, diabetes, thyroid disorders, and adrenal dysfunction.

What is a Hormone?

A hormone is a chemical messenger released into the bloodstream by endocrine cells. Hormones bind to specific receptors on target cells and change cellular activity.

Endocrine vs Exocrine

EndocrineExocrine
Released into bloodReleased through ducts
Acts on distant cellsActs locally
Examples: insulin, thyroid hormoneExamples: saliva, sweat, enzymes

Classes of Hormones

  • Peptide/Protein: Bind membrane receptors, act quickly, short duration
  • Steroid: Bind intracellular receptors, slow onset, long-lasting effects

Goal of Hormones

Hormones regulate enzyme activity, protein synthesis, membrane transport, growth, and metabolism in order to maintain homeostasis.

Half-Life

Half-life is the time required for hormone concentration to decrease by 50%. It affects medication dosing, lab timing, and how long hormone effects last. Steroid hormones tend to last longer due to carrier proteins.

Hormone Waves

First wave: TRH, ADH, GnRH, GHRH, CRH

Second wave: TSH, Prolactin, LH/FSH, GH, ACTH

Third wave: T3/T4, Testosterone, Estrogen/Progesterone, Cortisol

Clinical Connections

  • ADH regulates hydration and blood pressure.
  • Low thyroid hormone can cause fatigue and cold intolerance.
  • High cortisol can lead to weight gain and immune suppression.
  • Lab values are used to monitor endocrine health.
