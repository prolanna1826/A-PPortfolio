Body System Page

Digestive System

Overview

Major Functions of the Digestive System

  1. Ingestion – Taking food into the mouth.
  2. Mechanical digestion – Physically breaking food into smaller pieces (chewing, churning).
  3. Chemical digestion – Enzymatic breakdown of macromolecules into absorbable units.
  4. Absorption – Movement of nutrients, water, vitamins, and minerals into blood or lymph.
  5. Elimination – Removal of indigestible waste as feces.

Clinical Relevance

Impaired digestion can lead to malnutrition, anemia, vitamin deficiencies, dehydration, and electrolyte imbalance. A blocked bile duct can prevent fat digestion and cause pale stool.

Nursing Connection

Monitor bowel movements, stool color, abdominal pain, weight changes, hydration status, and signs of malnutrition.

Anatomical Organization

Location

The digestive system extends from the mouth to the anus. The esophagus passes through the mediastinum, and most digestive organs are located within the abdominal cavity.

Key Anatomical Components

  • Mouth
  • Pharynx
  • Esophagus
  • Stomach
  • Small intestine (duodenum, jejunum, ileum)
  • Large intestine
  • Accessory organs (liver, gallbladder, pancreas)

Clinical Relevance

Esophageal disorders like GERD occur in the mediastinum. Pelvic structures include the sigmoid colon, rectum, and anal canal.

Nursing Connection

Pain location and abdominal quadrant assessment help identify which organ is affected.

Anatomically Similar Structures

  • Rugae vs plicae circulares
  • Villi vs microvilli
  • Internal vs external sphincters

Clinical Relevance

Damage to villi (such as in celiac disease) leads to malabsorption and weight loss.

Mechanical vs Chemical Digestion

Mechanical Digestion

Physical breakdown of food.

Occurs in mouth (mastication), stomach (churning), small intestine (segmentation).

Example: Teeth crushing food.

Chemical Digestion

Enzymatic breakdown of food molecules.

Occurs in mouth, stomach, and small intestine.

Example: Amylase breaking starch into maltose.

Clinical Relevance

Pancreatic insufficiency reduces chemical digestion, leading to steatorrhea.

Nursing Connection

Assess for bloating, greasy stools, diarrhea, and nutrient deficiencies.

Digestive Tract Breakdown

  1. Mouth

    Features: Teeth, tongue, salivary glands

    Secretions: Salivary amylase

    Substrate: Starch → maltose

    Mechanical: Mastication

    Clinical Relevance

    Poor dentition affects nutrition.

    Nursing Connection

    Encourage oral care and adequate chewing.

  2. Pharynx / Esophagus

    Features: Stratified squamous epithelium

    Secretions: Mucus

    Action: Peristalsis

    Mechanical: Swallowing

    Clinical Relevance

    GERD damages lower esophageal sphincter.

    Nursing Connection

    Monitor for dysphagia and reflux symptoms.

  3. Stomach

    Features: Rugae, gastric pits, three muscle layers

    Secretions: HCl, pepsin, intrinsic factor

    Substrate: Protein → peptides

    Mechanical: Churning

    Clinical Relevance

    Ulcers may result from H. pylori or NSAID use.

    Nursing Connection

    Monitor for GI bleeding (black stool).

  4. Small Intestine

    Features: Villi, plicae circulares, microvilli

    Secretions: Pancreatic enzymes, bile

    Substrate: Carbohydrates, proteins, fats

    Mechanical: Segmentation

    Clinical Relevance

    Primary site of nutrient absorption.

    Nursing Connection

    Monitor for diarrhea and malabsorption signs.

  5. Liver / Gallbladder / Pancreas

    Liver produces bile and metabolizes nutrients.

    Gallbladder stores bile.

    Pancreas releases digestive enzymes and bicarbonate.

    Clinical Relevance

    Gallstones can block bile flow and cause jaundice.

    Nursing Connection

    Monitor for right upper quadrant pain and yellowing of skin.

  6. Large Intestine

    Features: No villi, many goblet cells

    Function: Water absorption

    Mechanical: Haustral contractions

    Clinical Relevance

    Dehydration alters stool consistency.

    Nursing Connection

    Monitor hydration and bowel patterns.

  7. Rectum / Anus

    Function: Stores feces

    Sphincters: Internal (involuntary), external (voluntary)

    Clinical Relevance

    Hemorrhoids and incontinence.

    Nursing Connection

    Assess bowel habits and control.

Digestive Enzymes

  • Salivary amylase

    Source: Salivary glands

    Site of action: Mouth

    Substrate: Starch

    Product: Maltose

  • Pepsin

    Source: Stomach

    Site of action: Stomach

    Substrate: Protein

    Product: Peptides

  • Pancreatic amylase

    Source: Pancreas

    Site of action: Small intestine

    Substrate: Starch

    Product: Maltose

  • Trypsin / Chymotrypsin

    Source: Pancreas

    Site of action: Small intestine

    Substrate: Protein

    Product: Peptides

  • Pancreatic lipase

    Source: Pancreas

    Site of action: Small intestine

    Substrate: Triglycerides

    Product: Fatty acids + monoglycerides

  • Lactase / Sucrase / Maltase

    Source: Small intestine

    Site of action: Brush border

    Substrate: Disaccharides

    Product: Monosaccharides

  • Bile (not enzyme)

    Source: Liver

    Site of action: Small intestine

    Function: Emulsifies fat

Brush Border Enzymes

Brush border enzymes are embedded in the microvilli of the small intestine and complete the final steps of chemical digestion.

  • Lactase

    Substrate: Lactose

    Product: Glucose + galactose

    Function: Digests milk sugar

  • Sucrase

    Substrate: Sucrose

    Product: Glucose + fructose

    Function: Breaks down table sugar

  • Maltase

    Substrate: Maltose

    Product: Two glucose molecules

    Function: Converts maltose to glucose

  • Peptidases (aminopeptidase, dipeptidyl peptidase)

    Substrate: Small peptides

    Product: Amino acids

    Function: Final protein breakdown

  • Enteropeptidase (enterokinase)

    Substrate: Trypsinogen

    Product: Trypsin

    Function: Activates trypsinogen → trypsin

  • Nucleotidases / nucleosidases

    Substrate: Nucleotides

    Product: Nitrogen bases + sugars

    Function: Break down nucleic acids

Clinical Relevance

Damage to microvilli (celiac disease) reduces these enzymes and causes diarrhea and nutrient deficiencies.

Nursing Connection

Monitor weight, stool pattern, and signs of malabsorption.

Digestive Hormones

  • CCK

    Source: Duodenum

    Target/action: Stimulates gallbladder contraction and pancreatic enzyme release

    Effect: Promotes fat and protein digestion

  • Secretin

    Source: Duodenum

    Target/action: Stimulates bicarbonate secretion from pancreas

    Effect: Neutralizes acidic chyme

  • Leptin

    Source: Adipose tissue

    Effect: Decreases appetite

  • Ghrelin

    Source: Stomach

    Effect: Increases hunger

  • Insulin

    Source: Pancreas

    Effect: Lowers blood glucose

  • Glucagon

    Source: Pancreas

    Effect: Raises blood glucose

Clinical Relevance

Dysregulation leads to diabetes or obesity.

Nursing Connection

Monitor blood glucose and dietary intake.

Liver & Pancreas Roles

  • Liver

    Digestion role: Produces bile for fat emulsification.

    Metabolism role: Converts glucose, detoxifies substances, synthesizes plasma proteins.

    Blood supply for absorbed nutrients: Hepatic portal vein.

  • Pancreas

    Exocrine: Releases digestive enzymes and bicarbonate into duodenum.

    Endocrine: Releases insulin and glucagon into bloodstream.

Cellular Respiration

Purpose: To produce ATP from glucose for cellular energy.

Overall reaction:

C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + 30–32 ATP

  1. Glycolysis

    Location: Cytoplasm

    Key events: Glucose split into 2 pyruvate

    Products: 2 ATP + NADH

  2. Intermediate Stage

    Location: Mitochondria

    Key events: Pyruvate converted to acetyl-CoA

    Products: CO2 + NADH

  3. Krebs Cycle

    Location: Mitochondria

    Key events: Acetyl-CoA broken down

    Products: NADH, FADH2, ATP

  4. Electron Transport Chain

    Location: Inner mitochondrial membrane

    Key events: Electrons transferred, oxygen final acceptor

    Products: Majority of ATP

Clinical Relevance

Hypoxia stops ATP production.

Nursing Connection

Monitor oxygenation and perfusion.

Intermolecular Interconversion

Definition: Conversion of nutrients into other molecules based on body needs.

  • Carbs → fats = Lipogenesis
  • Fats → ketones = Ketogenesis
  • Proteins → glucose = Gluconeogenesis

Clinical Relevance

Seen in fasting and diabetes.

Nursing Connection

Monitor ketone levels in diabetic patients.

Nitrogen Balance

  • Positive nitrogen balance

    Definition: Nitrogen intake exceeds loss

    Example: Growth, pregnancy

  • Negative nitrogen balance

    Definition: Nitrogen loss exceeds intake

    Example: Trauma, infection, starvation

Clinical Relevance

Indicates muscle wasting.

Nursing Connection

Monitor protein intake in hospitalized patients.

Hormonal Regulation of Blood Glucose

  • Insulin

    Action: Increases glucose uptake and storage

    Effect: Lowers blood glucose

  • Glucagon

    Action: Stimulates glycogen breakdown

    Effect: Raises blood glucose

  • Epinephrine

    Action: Stimulates glycogen breakdown

    Effect: Raises blood glucose

  • Cortisol

    Action: Stimulates gluconeogenesis

    Effect: Raises blood glucose

Review Questions

  1. What is the role of brush border enzymes?

    Brush border enzymes complete final digestion into absorbable units.

  2. How do CCK and secretin regulate digestion?

    CCK stimulates bile and enzymes; secretin neutralizes acid.

  3. Explain how nutrients are absorbed and delivered to the liver.

    Nutrients absorbed in small intestine enter capillaries → hepatic portal vein → liver.

  4. What are the steps of cellular respiration?

    Cellular respiration includes glycolysis, Krebs cycle, and ETC.

  5. What is nitrogen balance, and why does it matter?

    Nitrogen balance reflects protein intake vs loss and indicates nutritional status.
