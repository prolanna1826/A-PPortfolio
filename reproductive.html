Reproductive System

Overview

Major functions of the reproductive system

  1. The reproductive system produces gametes, which are specialized sex cells required for reproduction. In males these gametes are sperm cells, and in females they are oocytes (eggs).
  2. The reproductive system produces and secretes sex hormones such as testosterone, estrogen, and progesterone. These hormones regulate sexual development, reproductive cycles, fertility, and secondary sex characteristics.
  3. The reproductive system allows fertilization, supports fetal development during pregnancy, and ultimately enables childbirth and continuation of the human species.

From a healthcare perspective, the reproductive system also influences many other body systems including bone density, metabolism, cardiovascular health, and endocrine regulation.

Male & Female Anatomy (Compare & Contrast)

A. Major Anatomical Structures

  • Gonads

    Male: The male gonads are the testes. They produce sperm through spermatogenesis and secrete the hormone testosterone.

    Female: The female gonads are the ovaries. They produce oocytes through oogenesis and secrete the hormones estrogen and progesterone.

    Similarities / Differences: Both testes and ovaries produce gametes and hormones that regulate reproduction. The major difference is that males produce sperm continuously after puberty, while females are born with a limited number of oocytes.

  • Duct system

    Male: The male duct system includes the epididymis, vas deferens, ejaculatory ducts, and urethra. These structures transport sperm from the testes to the exterior of the body.

    Female: The female duct system includes the uterine tubes (fallopian tubes), uterus, cervix, and vagina. These structures transport oocytes, allow fertilization, and support embryo development.

    Similarities / Differences: Both systems contain ducts that transport reproductive cells, but in females the ducts also support fertilization and fetal development.

  • External genitalia

    Male: The male external genitalia include the penis and scrotum. The penis delivers sperm during ejaculation, and the scrotum houses the testes and regulates their temperature.

    Female: The female external genitalia are collectively called the vulva and include the labia majora, labia minora, clitoris, and vaginal opening.

    Similarities / Differences: Both sets of structures play roles in sexual function and reproduction, but male genitalia mainly deliver sperm while female genitalia receive sperm and protect reproductive organs.

  • Urethra

    Male: In males the urethra serves both urinary and reproductive functions because it carries urine and semen.

    Female: In females the urethra only transports urine and is not involved in reproduction.

    Similarities / Differences: The female urethra is shorter and only part of the urinary system, while the male urethra is longer and shared between urinary and reproductive systems.

B. Urethral Structures

  • Male urethra (prostatic → membranous → spongy)

    The male urethra has three main regions. The prostatic urethra passes through the prostate gland and receives secretions from the reproductive ducts. The membranous urethra passes through the urogenital diaphragm and is the shortest section. The spongy urethra runs through the penis and opens at the external urethral orifice.

  • Female urethra (length, function, location)

    The female urethra is relatively short, approximately 3–4 centimeters long. It runs from the urinary bladder to the external urethral opening located between the clitoris and the vaginal opening. Its primary function is transporting urine from the bladder to the outside of the body.

  • Comparison (function + anatomy)

    The male urethra is longer and serves both urinary and reproductive roles, while the female urethra is shorter and only involved in urination. Because the female urethra is shorter, females are more susceptible to urinary tract infections.

Gonadotropin Regulation (Male & Female)

A. Hypothalamic–Pituitary–Gonadal Axis

Hypothalamus releases:

  • Gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH)

Anterior pituitary releases:

  • FSH: Follicle-stimulating hormone stimulates gamete production in both males and females.
  • LH: Luteinizing hormone stimulates sex hormone production in the gonads.

This regulatory pathway is called the hypothalamic–pituitary–gonadal axis (HPG axis) and is controlled by negative feedback from sex hormones.

B. Male Effects

  • FSH → stimulates Sertoli cells in the seminiferous tubules, supporting sperm production.
  • LH → stimulates Leydig cells in the testes to produce testosterone.

C. Female Effects

  • FSH → stimulates ovarian follicle development and estrogen production.
  • LH → triggers ovulation and formation of the corpus luteum.

Gametogenesis (Compare Male & Female)

  • Location

    Male: Spermatogenesis occurs in the seminiferous tubules of the testes.

    Female: Oogenesis occurs in the ovaries.

  • Starting cell

    Male: Spermatogonia (diploid stem cells)

    Female: Oogonia that develop into primary oocytes.

  • Final cell produced

    Male: Spermatozoa (sperm cells)

    Female: Mature ovum (egg cell)

  • Number of functional gametes

    Male: Four functional sperm cells are produced from one primary spermatocyte.

    Female: One functional ovum is produced along with three polar bodies.

  • Timing

    Male: Spermatogenesis begins at puberty and continues throughout life.

    Female: Oogenesis begins before birth, pauses until puberty, and continues cyclically until menopause.

Male Reproductive System

A. Testicular Anatomy

  • Seminiferous tubules → microscopic tubes where spermatogenesis occurs.
  • Epididymis → long coiled duct where sperm mature and gain motility.
  • Vas deferens → muscular tube that transports sperm from the epididymis to the ejaculatory duct.
  • Spermatic cord → structure containing the vas deferens, blood vessels, nerves, and lymphatic vessels that support the testes.

B. Accessory Glands

  • Seminal vesicles

    Secretion: Fructose-rich fluid, prostaglandins, and clotting proteins.

    Function: Provides energy for sperm and makes up most of the semen volume.

  • Prostate gland

    Secretion: Slightly acidic fluid containing enzymes and prostate-specific antigen.

    Function: Activates sperm and enhances motility.

  • Bulbourethral glands (Cowper’s glands)

    Secretion: Clear mucus-like fluid.

    Function: Lubricates the urethra and neutralizes acidic urine residue before ejaculation.

C. Sperm Production

  • Sperm production rate per day:

    Approximately 100–300 million sperm are produced daily in a healthy adult male.

  • Temperature regulation (cremaster + dartos):

    The cremaster muscle raises or lowers the testes in response to temperature changes, while the dartos muscle wrinkles the scrotal skin to conserve heat. These mechanisms keep the testes slightly cooler than body temperature, which is necessary for sperm production.

Female Reproductive System

A. Ovarian Maturation Process

Primordial follicle → Primary follicle → Secondary follicle → Mature (Graafian) follicle → Ovulation

Corpus luteum function:

After ovulation, the remaining follicle becomes the corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone and estrogen to maintain the uterine lining and prepare the uterus for possible pregnancy.

B. Ovarian Cycle

  • Follicular Phase

    Key events: Follicles mature and estrogen levels increase.

    Hormones: FSH stimulates follicle growth and estrogen production.

  • Ovulation

    Key events: The mature follicle releases an oocyte from the ovary.

    Hormones: Triggered by a surge in luteinizing hormone (LH).

  • Luteal Phase

    Key events: Corpus luteum forms and secretes progesterone.

    Hormones: Progesterone maintains the uterine lining for possible implantation.

C. Uterine (Menstrual) Cycle

  • Menstrual Phase

    Endometrial status: Shedding of the uterine lining resulting in menstruation.

    Hormones: Estrogen and progesterone levels are low.

  • Proliferative Phase

    Endometrial status: Endometrium thickens and regenerates.

    Hormones: Estrogen increases.

  • Secretory Phase

    Endometrial status: Endometrium becomes highly vascularized and prepared for implantation.

    Hormones: Progesterone dominates.

Sexual Function & Autonomic Nervous System

  • Parasympathetic

    Male: Causes penile erection through vasodilation.

    Female: Promotes vaginal lubrication and clitoral erection.

  • Sympathetic

    Male: Controls ejaculation.

    Female: Contributes to uterine contractions and orgasm.

Pregnancy & Early Development

Hormonal Maintenance of Pregnancy

  • Corpus luteum produces progesterone to maintain the uterine lining.
  • Early embryo releases human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), which maintains the corpus luteum.
  • The placenta forms and eventually takes over progesterone production to maintain pregnancy.

Key Developmental Steps

Early Embryo Formation

Key developmental step:

Neurulation (formation of the neural tube, which becomes the brain and spinal cord).

Why this matters:

The neural tube forms the central nervous system and is essential for proper brain and spinal cord development.

First Trimester

  • Neurulation

    Occurs around week 3–4 and forms the neural tube.

  • Placental formation

    Develops to support nutrient and gas exchange between mother and fetus.

  • Heartbeat begins

    The fetal heart begins beating around week 4–5.

Second Trimester

  • Organogenesis

    Major organs continue forming and becoming functional.

  • Growth and refinement

    The fetus grows rapidly and body systems continue to mature.

Third Trimester

  • Activation of alveolar type II cells

    These cells produce surfactant, which prevents alveoli from collapsing.

  • Final lung maturation

    Lungs prepare for breathing after birth.

Fetal Heart Development & Circulation

When does fetal heart activity begin?

Around week 4 of embryonic development.

Key differences from adult circulation:

  • Foramen ovale

    Opening between the atria that allows blood to bypass the lungs.

  • Ductus arteriosus

    Connects the pulmonary artery to the aorta to bypass lung circulation.

  • Umbilical vessels

    Umbilical vein carries oxygenated blood from placenta to fetus; umbilical arteries carry deoxygenated blood back to placenta.

Pregnancy Testing

Hormone used to confirm pregnancy:

Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG)

Source of this hormone:

Produced by the developing embryo and later by the placenta.

Review Questions

  1. Compare urethras in males vs females

    The male urethra is longer and serves both urinary and reproductive functions, while the female urethra is shorter and only carries urine.

  2. What do FSH and LH do in each sex?

    In males, FSH stimulates sperm production and LH stimulates testosterone production. In females, FSH stimulates follicle development and estrogen production, while LH triggers ovulation and formation of the corpus luteum.

  3. Compare spermatogenesis and oogenesis

    Spermatogenesis produces four sperm cells continuously after puberty, while oogenesis produces one functional ovum per cycle and begins before birth.

  4. Describe the ovarian cycle and how it relates to the uterine cycle

    The ovarian cycle regulates follicle development, ovulation, and corpus luteum formation, while the uterine cycle prepares the endometrium for possible implantation.

  5. Explain how pregnancy is hormonally maintained through the first trimester

    During early pregnancy the corpus luteum produces progesterone, but the embryo releases hCG to keep the corpus luteum functioning until the placenta takes over hormone production.

  6. When does the fetal heart begin beating?

    Around the fourth week of development.

  7. How does fetal circulation differ from adult circulation?

    Fetal circulation bypasses the lungs using the foramen ovale, ductus arteriosus, and umbilical vessels because oxygen comes from the placenta instead of the lungs.

  8. What hormone confirms pregnancy?

    Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG).

  9. What is the key developmental step that starts embryonic formation?

    Neurulation, which forms the neural tube that develops into the brain and spinal cord.
