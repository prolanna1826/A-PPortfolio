Skeletal System

Overview

The skeletal system provides the body with structure, support, and protection while also helping with movement, mineral storage, blood cell production, and energy storage. Bones work together with muscles and joints to make movement possible, and they also serve as a major reservoir for calcium and phosphorus. Even though bones look rigid, they are living tissues that constantly grow, remodel, and respond to hormones, nutrition, and mechanical stress.

Major functions of the skeletal system:

1. Support – provides the framework of the body and supports soft tissues.
2. Protection – protects organs such as the brain, spinal cord, heart, and lungs.
3. Movement – acts as a system of levers for skeletal muscles.
4. Mineral storage – stores calcium and phosphorus for later use.
5. Blood cell production – red bone marrow produces red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.
6. Energy storage – yellow bone marrow stores triglycerides.

Bone Shapes

Bones are classified by shape, and each shape is related to a specific function. Knowing these categories helps explain why certain bones are better suited for support, protection, or movement.

Bone Shape Definition / Key Features Examples
Long bones Longer than they are wide, with a shaft called a diaphysis and ends called epiphyses. These bones are especially important for movement and weight bearing. Femur, humerus, tibia
Short bones Roughly cube-shaped bones that provide stability and support with limited motion. Carpals, tarsals
Flat bones Thin, flattened, and often slightly curved bones that protect internal organs and provide a broad surface for muscle attachment. Sternum, ribs, cranial bones
Irregular bones Bones with more complex shapes that do not fit into the other groups. Vertebrae, sphenoid, mandible
Sesamoid bones Small round bones that form inside tendons, usually where a tendon passes over a joint. Patella
Sutural bones Small extra bones found within the sutures of the skull. Wormian bones

Bone Cells

Bone tissue is constantly maintained by specialized cells. These cells work together to grow bone, maintain it, and break it down when remodeling is needed.

Cell Type Function Notes
Osteoprogenitor cells Stem cells that divide and develop into osteoblasts. Found in the periosteum and endosteum and important for growth and repair.
Osteoblasts Bone-forming cells that secrete osteoid, the organic part of bone matrix. Active during bone growth, fracture repair, and bone deposition.
Osteocytes Mature bone cells that maintain the bone matrix. Located in lacunae and connected through canaliculi.
Osteoclasts Bone-resorbing cells that break down bone tissue. Large multinucleated cells that release calcium into the blood.

Microscopic Structure of Compact Bone

Compact bone is organized into units called osteons, also known as Haversian systems. Each osteon is built for strength, support, and the movement of nutrients through bone tissue.

Important structures of an osteon:

Central (Haversian) canal – contains blood vessels and nerves.
Lamellae – rings or layers of bone matrix arranged around the central canal. These include concentric, interstitial, and circumferential lamellae.
Lacunae with osteocytes – small spaces that contain mature bone cells.
Canaliculi – tiny channels that connect lacunae and allow communication and nutrient movement.
Periosteum – the outer connective tissue covering of bone.

Ossification Processes

Ossification is the process of bone formation. There are two main ways bones develop in the body.

Process Steps Example Bones
Intramembranous ossification Bone develops directly from mesenchymal tissue. Mesenchymal cells become osteoblasts, osteoblasts secrete matrix, and the matrix mineralizes. Flat bones of the skull, part of the clavicle
Endochondral ossification Bone develops by replacing a hyaline cartilage model. This process includes a cartilage model, primary ossification center, secondary ossification centers, and growth at the epiphyseal plate. Femur, tibia, humerus, and most other long bones

Hormonal Regulation of Bone Remodeling

Bone remodeling is strongly influenced by hormones. These hormones affect osteoblast and osteoclast activity, which changes whether bone is deposited or resorbed.

Hormone Effect on Osteoblasts Effect on Osteoclasts Net Effect on Bone
Parathyroid hormone (PTH) Indirectly shifts balance away from deposition Increases osteoclast activity indirectly Increases bone resorption and raises blood calcium
Calcitonin Supports osteoblast activity Decreases osteoclast activity Promotes calcium storage in bone
Vitamin D (calcitriol) Supports mineralization when calcium is available Can indirectly support remodeling Increases calcium absorption and supports healthy bone formation
Growth hormone Stimulates osteoblasts and bone growth No major direct effect Promotes bone growth, especially during development
Sex hormones (estrogen/testosterone) Help maintain osteoblast activity Reduce osteoclast activity Maintain bone density and reduce bone loss

Calcium and Physiology

Calcium is one of the most important minerals stored in bone, but it also has major functions in the nervous, muscular, and circulatory systems.

Neurotransmitter exocytosis: Calcium enters the presynaptic terminal and triggers synaptic vesicles to fuse with the membrane and release neurotransmitters.
Hemostasis: Calcium acts as an essential cofactor in multiple steps of the clotting cascade.
Muscle contraction: Calcium binds to troponin, moves tropomyosin, and allows myosin heads to attach to actin so contraction can occur.

Joints

Joints are places where two or more bones meet. They are classified by both structure and function.

Structural Classification

Type Description Example
Fibrous Bones are joined by dense connective tissue and there is no joint cavity. Skull sutures
Cartilaginous Bones are joined by cartilage and allow little movement. Intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis
Synovial Bones are separated by a fluid-filled joint cavity and allow the greatest range of motion. Shoulder, knee, elbow

Functional Classification

Type Description Example
Synarthrosis Immovable joint Skull sutures
Amphiarthrosis Slightly movable joint Pubic symphysis
Diarthrosis Freely movable joint Most synovial joints, such as the shoulder

Clinical Relevance

Post-menopausal osteoporosis involves decreased estrogen levels, which increases bone resorption and lowers bone density. This makes bones weaker and increases the risk of fractures, especially in the hip, vertebrae, and wrist.

Rickets in children results from vitamin D deficiency. Without enough vitamin D, calcium absorption decreases and the bones become soft, weak, and more likely to bend or deform.

Review Questions

1. Which hormones increase bone resorption, and which promote bone deposition?

Parathyroid hormone increases bone resorption. Calcitonin, growth hormone, and sex hormones promote bone deposition or help preserve bone mass.

2. What structural differences exist between compact and spongy bone?

Compact bone is dense and organized into osteons. Spongy bone is less dense, contains trabeculae, and has spaces that often contain marrow.

3. Compare the sequence of ossification in long bones versus flat bones.

Long bones form by endochondral ossification, where bone replaces a cartilage model. Flat bones form by intramembranous ossification, where bone develops directly from mesenchymal tissue.

4. Explain how calcium is essential to the nervous, muscular, and circulatory systems.

Calcium is necessary for neurotransmitter release, muscle contraction, and blood clotting.

5. Which joint type allows the most movement? Provide an example.

Synovial joints allow the most movement. One example is the shoulder joint.
