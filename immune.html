IMMUNITY + INFECTIOUS AGENTS STUDY NOTES (WITH NURSING CONNECTIONS)
Key Terms

Pathogen
A microorganism that causes disease.
What it really means: A pathogen is any microbe (bacteria, virus, fungus, parasite) that can invade your body, multiply, and cause tissue damage or dysfunction. Some microbes live on us normally and don’t cause harm (normal flora). A pathogen is a “problem microbe” because it can overwhelm defenses or produce toxins.
Clinical / nursing relevance: Nurses care because the suspected pathogen changes everything: isolation precautions, treatment type, and urgency. For example, bacterial pneumonia needs antibiotics, while viral flu needs supportive care and possibly antivirals. Also, immunocompromised patients can develop infections from organisms that usually don’t cause disease.

Antigen
A molecule that triggers an immune response.
What it really means: An antigen is basically the “ID tag” the immune system recognizes. It can be on the surface of a bacteria, part of a virus, a toxin, or even an abnormal self-cell (like cancer). Antigens are often proteins or polysaccharides.
Clinical / nursing relevance: Vaccines work by exposing the immune system to an antigen safely, so memory cells form. Antigen recognition is also why blood transfusions can cause reactions (ABO and Rh antigens are literally antigens).

Antibody
A Y-shaped protein that binds to a specific antigen.
What it really means: Antibodies (immunoglobulins) are made by plasma cells (activated B cells). They bind antigens like a lock-and-key. Antibodies don’t usually “kill” directly. They neutralize (block), tag invaders for phagocytosis (opsonization), and activate complement.
Clinical / nursing relevance: Antibodies explain immunity after infection/vaccine and also explain allergic reactions and autoimmune disease patterns. Antibody testing (titers, serology) is used clinically to check past exposure, immunity, and some active infections.

APC (Antigen-Presenting Cell)
A cell that engulfs and presents antigens to T cells.
What it really means: APCs are the bridge between innate and adaptive immunity. They phagocytose a pathogen, process it into fragments, and display fragments on MHC molecules so T cells can recognize it. Without APCs doing this “presentation,” T cells won’t activate properly.
Clinical / nursing relevance: APC function is critical in immunocompromised patients. If antigen presentation is weak, patients get recurrent infections, slower recovery, and higher complication risk. This is why patients on immunosuppressants, chemo, or steroids need infection monitoring and prevention education.

Cytokine
A chemical messenger that coordinates immune activity.
What it really means: Cytokines are immune communication signals. They tell cells when to activate, move, multiply, cause inflammation, or stop. Important cytokine families include interleukins (IL), interferons (IFN), and TNF.
Clinical / nursing relevance: Cytokines are behind fever and inflammation. Too many cytokines can cause systemic inflammatory responses (like in sepsis), which is why nurses monitor vitals, perfusion, mental status, and labs closely. Too little cytokine activity → weak immune defense.

MHC I
Displays endogenous (intracellular) antigens to CD8+ cytotoxic T cells.
What it really means: MHC I is on almost all nucleated cells. It shows “inside-the-cell” peptides (like viral proteins or abnormal cancer proteins). If something looks wrong, CD8+ T cells kill the cell.
Clinical / nursing relevance: This is why cytotoxic T cells are essential for controlling viral infections and cancer. If CD8 function is suppressed (HIV, transplant meds), viral infections can become severe or reactivated.

MHC II
Displays exogenous (extracellular) antigens to CD4+ helper T cells.
What it really means: MHC II is only on professional APCs (macrophages, dendritic cells, B cells). It shows peptides from pathogens that were engulfed from outside the cell. This activates helper T cells, which then coordinate the immune response.
Clinical / nursing relevance: If MHC II presentation is weak, adaptive immunity is weak overall. Helper T cells control B cell antibody production and CD8 activation, so MHC II issues can create broad immune problems.

Complement System
A cascade of proteins that enhances immune defense.
What it really means: Complement is a group of plasma proteins that activate in a chain reaction. It boosts immune response by tagging pathogens, recruiting immune cells, and forming pores that can lyse cells.
Clinical / nursing relevance: Complement is part of inflammation and tissue damage when over-activated. In severe infections, complement contributes to systemic inflammation. Nurses monitor for sepsis signs and worsening inflammation. Complement deficiencies can cause recurrent infections.

Infectious Agents and Immunity 
Bacteria
Structure: Prokaryotic cells, usually with a cell wall (peptidoglycan), reproduce on their own.
Example: Streptococcus (strep throat), E. coli (UTI).
Treatment / nursing relevance: Antibiotics can treat bacterial infections. Nurses monitor for antibiotic allergies, side effects (GI upset, C. diff risk), and resistance. Education matters: finishing the course reduces resistant survivors. Labs like WBC and neutrophils often increase.

Viruses
Structure: Not a cell. Genetic material (DNA or RNA) inside a protein coat, sometimes with an envelope. Must enter a host cell to replicate.
Example: Influenza, COVID, RSV.
Treatment / nursing relevance: Antibiotics do not work. Treatment is supportive plus antivirals in some cases and prevention through vaccination. Nurses teach symptom management, hydration, fever care, and infection control. Lymphocytes may be more elevated compared to bacterial infections.

Fungus / Parasite
Structure:
Fungi are eukaryotic organisms (yeast like Candida or molds). Parasites are eukaryotic organisms that live in or on a host (protozoa like Giardia or helminths like worms).
Examples:
Fungus: Candida (thrush, vaginal yeast), Aspergillus (lung issues in immunocompromised).
Parasite: Giardia (diarrhea), Plasmodium (malaria).
Treatment / nursing relevance: Antifungals or antiparasitics are used, not antibiotics. Fungal infections often become serious in immunocompromised patients (opportunistic infections). Nurses watch for white patches (thrush), persistent cough in high-risk patients, GI symptoms, dehydration, and travel or exposure history. Prevention education is big: hygiene, safe water, food safety, and medication adherence.

What is the difference between viral and bacterial infections?
Bacterial infections are caused by living single-celled organisms that can multiply independently and often respond to antibiotics. Viral infections are caused by viruses that must use host cells to replicate, and antibiotics do not treat them. Clinically, this matters because giving antibiotics for a viral illness does not help the patient and contributes to antibiotic resistance. Nurses also look at patterns: bacterial infections may cause localized pus and neutrophilia, while viral infections often cause fatigue, body aches, and can show increased lymphocytes. But symptoms overlap, so clinical judgment + testing (cultures, PCR, antigen tests) matters.

Immune Cells Overview 
Dendritic Cell
Location: Skin and mucosa, also lymph nodes after they migrate.
Function: Captures antigens, travels to lymph nodes, presents antigen to T cells (strongest APC).
Clinical connection: Key antigen-presenting cell.
Nursing relevance: Dendritic cells are the reason vaccines work well. If a patient is immunosuppressed, T-cell activation may be weaker, so infections can be harder to clear.

Mast Cell
Location: Connective tissues, near blood vessels, skin, respiratory tract, GI tract.
Function: Releases histamine and other mediators (inflammation, allergy).
Clinical connection: Involved in allergic reactions.
Nursing relevance: Mast cells explain hives, swelling, bronchoconstriction, and anaphylaxis. Nurses monitor airway, breathing, BP, and administer emergency meds (epinephrine) when severe.

Macrophage
Location: Tissues (not bloodstream).
Function: Phagocytosis, cleanup of debris, antigen presentation (APC), cytokine release.
Clinical connection: Engulfs pathogens and debris.
Nursing relevance: Macrophages drive inflammation and can contribute to fever via cytokines. They’re also important for wound healing and infection control.

Microglia
Location: Central nervous system (brain/spinal cord).
Function: Immune defense in nervous tissue (phagocytosis, cytokines).
Clinical connection: Immune defense of brain tissue.
Nursing relevance: Microglia activation is involved in neuroinflammation. In CNS infections, monitoring neuro status is critical (LOC changes, headache, neck stiffness).

Complement System
Classical Pathway (triggered by)
Triggered by antigen–antibody complexes (usually IgG or IgM bound to antigen).
Nursing relevance: This ties complement to adaptive immunity. It’s part of why antibodies can “call backup.”

Alternative Pathway (triggered by)
Triggered by pathogen surfaces directly (like bacterial cell walls).
Nursing relevance: This is innate immunity and works quickly even before antibodies are made.

C3 Activation → C3b → effects

C3 is a central complement protein. When activated, it produces C3b, which drives major outcomes.

Three main effects:

Opsonization: C3b coats pathogens and makes them easier for phagocytes to grab and destroy.
Nursing relevance: This supports quicker clearance. Patients with immune deficits may not opsonize well and get recurrent infections.

Cytolysis: Complement can form the membrane attack complex (MAC) that pokes holes in membranes and lyses cells.
Nursing relevance: Helpful against some bacteria but can contribute to inflammation and tissue damage when extreme.

Inflammation: Complement fragments (like C3a, C5a) recruit immune cells and increase vascular permeability.
Nursing relevance: This is part of redness, swelling, heat, pain. Too much systemic inflammation can worsen sepsis.

Fever and Inflammation 
Fever
Caused by release of: pyrogens (cytokines like IL-1, IL-6, TNF) acting on the hypothalamus.
Function of fever: Fever raises the body’s set point to help slow pathogen growth and enhance immune function. Some microbes replicate less efficiently at higher temperatures, and immune reactions can speed up.

Clinical note: Sustained high fever (> 104°F) can lead to complications like febrile seizures, dehydration, confusion, and possible organ stress.
Nursing relevance: Nurses monitor temperature trends, hydration status, mental status, and signs of sepsis. Fever is protective up to a point, but high or prolonged fever needs intervention and cause evaluation.

Inflammation (5 cardinal signs)

Redness (rubor)

Heat (calor)

Swelling (tumor)

Pain (dolor)

Loss of function (functio laesa)

Nursing relevance: Inflammation helps isolate and fight infection but also causes discomfort and tissue stress. Nurses assess inflammation as part of wound checks, IV site checks, and infection surveillance.

Antigens, Antibodies, and MHC Molecules 
Antigens are foreign molecules that elicit an immune response.
Antibodies are produced by plasma cells and bind to antigens to form an antigen–antibody complex.

Nursing relevance: Antigen–antibody interactions explain vaccine protection, allergy reactions, and autoimmune issues. It also explains transfusion reactions if blood antigens mismatch.

MHC Table 
MHC I
Found on: All nucleated cells
Presents to: CD8+ cytotoxic T cells
Antigen type: Endogenous (intracellular)
Example/relevance: Viral infections, cancer

MHC II
Found on: APCs (dendritic cells, macrophages, B cells)
Presents to: CD4+ helper T cells
Antigen type: Exogenous (extracellular)
Example/relevance: Bacterial infections, APC activation

Antigen-Presenting Cells (APCs)
Location: Tissues (lungs, liver, spleen, connective tissue, etc.)
Function: Phagocytosis of pathogens/debris; presents antigen on MHC II; releases cytokines to amplify inflammation.
Nursing relevance: Important in chronic inflammation and infection cleanup. In pneumonia, alveolar macrophages are a big deal.

Dendritic Cell
Location: Skin and mucosa; migrates to lymph nodes.
Function: Most powerful APC. Captures antigen, travels to lymph nodes, activates naive T cells.
Nursing relevance: Vaccine responses depend heavily on dendritic presentation.

B Cell
Location: Blood and lymph (lymph nodes, spleen).
Function: Presents antigen on MHC II to helper T cells; differentiates into plasma cells to make antibodies.
Nursing relevance: Low B-cell function increases risk for repeated bacterial infections; antibody-based immunity (vaccines, titers) depends on B cells.

T-Lymphocyte Activation — Helper (CD4+) T Cell Flow Chart 

Antigen-presenting cell (APC)
An APC (dendritic cell, macrophage, or B cell) has processed an antigen and is ready to show it.

Antigen displayed on MHC II molecule
The antigen fragment is displayed on the APC surface on an MHC II molecule.

Helper T cell receptor binds to antigen–MHC II complex
The T-cell receptor recognizes that specific antigen fragment presented by MHC II.

Co-stimulation via CD28
This is the “second signal” needed. Without co-stimulation, the T cell may become anergic (inactive), which prevents accidental overreaction.

Helper T cell activates and releases cytokines
Activated helper T cells release cytokines (like interleukins) that drive the immune response forward.

B cell and cytotoxic T cell activation
Cytokines stimulate B cells to become plasma cells (antibodies) and help activate CD8 cytotoxic T cells to kill infected cells.

Nursing relevance: CD4 cells are “immune coordinators.” If CD4 count/function is low (HIV), patients struggle with both antibody responses and cytotoxic responses and become vulnerable to opportunistic infections.

Cytotoxic (CD8+) T Cell Activation Pathway 

Infected cell presents antigen on MHC I
The infected cell displays intracellular antigen fragments on MHC I.

Cytotoxic T cell binds via its receptor
The CD8+ T cell receptor recognizes antigen–MHC I on the infected cell.

Activation triggers release of perforin and granzymes
Perforin forms pores in the target membrane. Granzymes enter and trigger apoptosis pathways.

Target cell lysis and apoptosis
The infected cell dies, stopping viral replication and removing abnormal cells.

Nursing note fill-in: Cytotoxic T cells are essential in controlling viral and cancer infections/conditions.
Nursing relevance: This is why transplant patients on immunosuppressants have higher cancer risk and severe viral infections. Monitoring for viral reactivation and unusual lesions is clinically important.

Guided Reflection 
1. How does understanding the immune system help you as a future nurse recognize early signs of infection?
Understanding the immune system helps me notice infection earlier because I’m not just looking for one obvious symptom like fever. I’m watching for patterns that show the body is starting an immune response. For example, inflammation signs like redness, warmth, swelling, and pain tell me immune cells are being recruited and blood vessels are becoming more permeable. Knowing cytokines drive fever helps me treat fever as a clue, not just a number, especially if the patient also has chills, tachycardia, or mental status changes. Immune knowledge also helps me connect lab values to what’s happening in the body, like neutrophils increasing more with many bacterial infections or lymphocytes increasing in some viral infections. Most importantly, if I understand how fast sepsis can develop when the immune response becomes systemic, I’m more likely to take early abnormal vitals seriously and escalate care quickly, instead of waiting until the patient “looks really bad.”

2. Why must nurses understand the difference between bacterial and viral infections before administering antibiotics?
Nurses need to understand this difference because antibiotics target structures or processes found in bacteria, not viruses. If a patient has a viral infection and receives antibiotics, the medication will not treat the cause, which delays proper care and gives the patient side effects without benefit. It also encourages antibiotic resistance by killing off sensitive bacteria in the body and allowing resistant strains to survive and spread. That becomes a patient safety issue long-term, not just “a public health thing.” In real nursing practice, this matters when patients request antibiotics for colds or flu symptoms. Being able to explain why antibiotics won’t help, while still validating their symptoms and focusing on supportive treatment, is part of good patient education. It also matters in hospitals where antibiotic stewardship programs try to prevent overuse and reduce C. difficile infections, allergic reactions, and medication interactions.

3. How do vaccinations make use of the immune system’s memory?
Vaccinations use immune memory by giving the immune system a safe “practice run” using an antigen that represents the real pathogen. When the vaccine antigen is introduced, APCs present it to helper T cells, which then activate B cells and sometimes cytotoxic T cells. B cells can differentiate into plasma cells that produce antibodies, but the most important long-term effect is that some B cells and T cells become memory cells. Those memory cells stay in the body for a long time. If the person is later exposed to the real pathogen, the immune system does not start from scratch. It responds faster and stronger, producing antibodies quickly and activating killer cells sooner, which can prevent infection entirely or at least make the illness much milder. This is why vaccines lower hospitalization and complication rates, especially in vulnerable populations like infants, elderly patients, and immunocompromised patients.
