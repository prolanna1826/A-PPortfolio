Nervous System

Overview

The nervous system is the body’s main communication and control system. It detects changes inside and outside the body, processes that information, and creates responses. It also plays a major role in homeostasis, movement, sensation, memory, and higher thinking. Because signals in the nervous system travel quickly, this system allows the body to react rapidly to the environment.

Major functions of the nervous system:

Sensory input
Integration of incoming information
Motor output to muscles and glands
Maintenance of homeostasis
Higher mental functions such as learning, memory, and emotion

Two main divisions:

Central Nervous System (CNS) = brain and spinal cord
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) = all nerves and ganglia outside the brain and spinal cord

Neurons (Functional Unit)

Neurons are the functional cells of nervous tissue. They receive, process, and transmit electrical signals.

Structure Function
Dendrites Receive incoming signals from sensory receptors or other neurons and carry them toward the cell body
Cell body (soma) Contains the nucleus and most organelles and acts as the metabolic center of the neuron
Axon Carries the action potential away from the cell body toward another neuron, muscle, or gland
Myelin sheath Insulates the axon and speeds up nerve impulse conduction
Nodes of Ranvier Gaps in the myelin sheath where the action potential is regenerated
Axon terminals Release neurotransmitters into the synapse

Neuroglia (Supporting Cells)

Neuroglial cells support, protect, nourish, and insulate neurons. They are not the primary signaling cells, but they are essential for normal nervous system function.

Cell Type Location Function
Astrocytes CNS Support neurons, help form the blood-brain barrier, and regulate the extracellular environment
Oligodendrocytes CNS Form myelin around CNS axons
Microglia CNS Act as phagocytic immune cells that remove pathogens and cellular debris
Ependymal cells CNS Line ventricles and central canal and help produce and circulate cerebrospinal fluid
Schwann cells PNS Form myelin around peripheral axons and help with repair of damaged peripheral nerves
Satellite cells PNS Surround neuron cell bodies in ganglia and help regulate the local environment

Graded Potentials vs. Action Potentials

Feature Graded Potential Action Potential
Location Dendrites and cell body Axon hillock, axon, and axon terminals
Trigger Stimulus or neurotransmitter binding Threshold reached at trigger zone
Spread Local and decreases with distance Propagates without losing strength
Summation Yes No
Threshold? No fixed threshold Yes, must be reached

Action Potential

1. Resting potential: Neurons are usually at about -70 mV, while skeletal muscle fibers are usually at about -90 mV. At rest, the inside of the cell is more negative than the outside.

2. Depolarization: Caused by influx of sodium (Na+) through voltage-gated channels.

3. Peak: The membrane potential rises to about +30 mV.

4. Repolarization: Caused by efflux of potassium (K+).

5. Hyperpolarization: The membrane potential drops below -70 mV for a short time.

6. Return to resting potential: Achieved through channel closing, leak channels, and the sodium-potassium pump.

Synaptic Transmission

Neurotransmitter is released from axon terminals / synaptic end bulbs by exocytosis. Calcium enters the presynaptic terminal and triggers synaptic vesicles to fuse with the membrane. The neurotransmitter then binds to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane, which causes a graded potential in the next cell.

Neurotransmitters and Functions

Neurotransmitter Excitatory/Inhibitory/Modulatory Major Functions
Acetylcholine (ACh) Excitatory or modulatory Skeletal muscle contraction, autonomic signaling, learning and memory
Norepinephrine (NE) Mostly excitatory/modulatory Alertness, attention, sympathetic responses, stress reactions
Dopamine (DA) Modulatory Reward, movement control, motivation, mood
Serotonin (5-HT) Mostly modulatory Mood, appetite, sleep, pain modulation
GABA Inhibitory Main inhibitory neurotransmitter in the CNS
Glutamate Excitatory Main excitatory neurotransmitter in the CNS; important in learning and memory

Regions of the Brain and Their Roles

Region Main Function
Prefrontal cortex (PFC) Decision-making, planning, judgment, working memory, personality
Hypothalamus Homeostasis, temperature control, hunger, thirst, endocrine regulation
Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) Attention, emotion, pain processing, decision-making
Primary visual cortex Receives and processes visual information
Primary somatosensory cortex Processes touch, pain, pressure, temperature, and body position
Primary motor cortex Initiates voluntary movement
Thalamus Relay station for sensory information heading to the cortex
Hippocampus Formation of new memories and spatial learning
Brainstem Controls breathing, heart rate, consciousness, and other basic life functions

Cranial Nerves

1. Olfactory (I) – sensory – smell
2. Optic (II) – sensory – vision
3. Oculomotor (III) – motor – eye movement and pupil constriction
4. Trochlear (IV) – motor – eye movement
5. Trigeminal (V) – both – facial sensation and chewing
6. Abducens (VI) – motor – eye movement
7. Facial (VII) – both – facial expression, taste, tears, saliva
8. Vestibulocochlear (VIII) – sensory – hearing and balance
9. Glossopharyngeal (IX) – both – taste, swallowing, salivation
10. Vagus (X) – both – autonomic control of thoracic and abdominal organs, swallowing, voice
11. Accessory (XI) – motor – neck and shoulder movement
12. Hypoglossal (XII) – motor – tongue movement

Choroid Plexus

Location: In the ventricles of the brain

Function: Produces cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)

Connection to CSF: It filters blood plasma to make CSF, which circulates through the ventricles, central canal, and subarachnoid space to protect and cushion the CNS.

Clinical Relevance

Stroke: Interruption of blood flow to brain tissue, causing loss of oxygen and neuronal death.

Parkinson’s disease: Degeneration of dopamine-producing neurons, especially in the substantia nigra, causing tremor, rigidity, and slowed movement.

Addiction: Changes in reward pathways, especially dopamine pathways, leading to craving, tolerance, and compulsive behavior.

Multiple sclerosis: Autoimmune destruction of myelin in the CNS, which slows or blocks nerve conduction.
