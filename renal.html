Renal System

Overview

The renal system maintains homeostasis by filtering blood, removing metabolic waste, balancing water and electrolytes, regulating acid-base status, and supporting blood pressure. It also has endocrine roles through renin release, erythropoietin production, and vitamin D activation.

Major Functions of the Urinary System

  • Excretion: removes urea, creatinine, uric acid, drugs, and toxins.
  • Water balance: adjusts urine concentration to protect blood volume and osmolality.
  • Electrolyte regulation: fine-tunes sodium, potassium, chloride, calcium, phosphate, and bicarbonate.
  • Acid-base control: handles hydrogen ion secretion and bicarbonate conservation.
  • Endocrine support: activates RAAS, stimulates RBC production, and supports bone health via calcitriol.

Key Concepts in Filtration

Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) is the amount of filtrate formed per minute and is one of the most useful clinical indicators of kidney function. GFR depends heavily on renal perfusion pressure and is influenced by autoregulation, tubuloglomerular feedback, and sympathetic tone.

  • Low perfusion can reduce GFR and increase AKI risk.
  • Macula densa signaling helps match filtration to tubular handling capacity.
  • Stress states can decrease renal blood flow to preserve systemic pressure.

Hormonal Control of Water and Blood Pressure

  • ADH: increases water reabsorption in collecting ducts and concentrates urine.
  • Aldosterone: increases sodium reabsorption and potassium secretion in distal nephron segments.
  • ANP: promotes sodium and water loss when blood volume is high.
  • RAAS: increases vasoconstriction, thirst, sodium retention, and blood pressure.

Acid-Base Regulation

Kidneys maintain pH by reabsorbing filtered bicarbonate, secreting hydrogen ions, and generating new bicarbonate. These mechanisms complement rapid respiratory CO2 regulation to stabilize systemic acid-base balance.

Clinical Connections

  • Falling urine output and rising creatinine can indicate worsening kidney perfusion or injury.
  • Electrolyte disturbances, especially potassium shifts, can cause dangerous arrhythmias.
  • SIADH and diabetes insipidus produce major changes in urine concentration and sodium balance.
  • ACE inhibitors and ARBs are kidney-protective in many conditions but require monitoring of potassium and creatinine.
