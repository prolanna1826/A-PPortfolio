Interview with a Professional

For this project I interviewed Guillermo A. Reyes, MD, FACC, FCCAI, diplomate of the American Board on Interventional Cardiology at Cardiology of San Antonio, P.A. about his path into medicine, what daily life in healthcare actually looks like, and what advice he has for students who want to work in cardiology. We discussed challenges of the profession, and realistic vision of the field.

Professional Info

Basic Details

  • Name:Guillermo A. Reyes, MD, FACC, FCCAI
  • Specialty:Cardiovascular Disease
  • Work setting:Diplomate of the American Board on Interventional Cardiology at Cardiology of San Antonio, P.A.
  • Years in practice:23+
  • Medical school:Baylor College of Medicine; Graduated 1991
  • Fellowship:Dr. Reyes is affiliated with Christus Santa Rosa Medical Center, Methodist Hospital and St Lukes Baptist Hospital.

Prior to interview

Why are you seeking to enter this field of work? What do you think will be your biggest strength and weakness in this occupation? What will be the most rewarding part of this job? What will be the most difficult aspect to handle?

I want to enter cardiology because the heart is one of the most important and critical systems in the body, and I like how cardiology combines critical thinking, fast decisions, lifestyle, and real patient impact. I am interested a field where small changes can mean a lot, and where strong knowledge can truly help save lives. It is one of the most problematic areas in the health field among American population, and I like that cardiology combine both scientific and personal aspects of the care, because heart problems affect not just the body, but a person’s whole life. I think my biggest strength would be that I am detail-oriented and serious when it comes to noticing and learning. In cardiology, that matters because you have to notice not just primary symptoms, but small symptoms, changes in lifestyle, behavior, and rhythm, lab values, and patient responses. My biggest weakness would probably be that I am usually overthinking/second-guessing myself and putting a lot of pressure on myself. The most rewarding part for me would be helping patients feel healthier and seeing that paycheck hitting my bank account. Talking from my experience, I know that the hardest part for me would be handling emotionally difficult cases and dealing with patient’s families.

Interview Questions

Why did you decide to be a cardiologist?

I ended up in cardiology because it’s one of those fields where you can’t fake your way through it. It is always busy out here, and it brings you very good money if you are good at what you are doing. You have to know what you’re doing, and your decisions matter fast. I liked that part of it. It’s a mix of physiology, procedures, long-term management, and high-stakes calls. It’s also a field where you can actually see whether your treatment is helping or not, sometimes immediately. *Dr. Reyes smiled.* After all these years, I still feel like I’m young enough to want the movement and the pressure too.

What is the most rewarding part of your job?

The most rewarding part is when you know you actually made a difference, not in some abstract way, but in a real one. A patient comes in scared, unstable, short of breath, in pain, and then later they’re better because you caught something, treated it correctly, or stepped in at the right time. That part never gets old. And I’ll tell you, when you’ve been in this long enough, you also start to appreciate the patients who trust you for years. That matters more than students usually realize. It’s not always the dramatic cases. Sometimes the reward is just helping someone stay alive, functional, and out of the hospital.

What is the hardest part of your job?

The hardest part is that the job does not stop being serious just because you’ve been doing it a long time. Heart disease is still heart disease. Patients are sick, sometimes noncompliant, sometimes they wait too long, sometimes the system slows everything down, and sometimes despite doing everything right, it is still bad. There’s also a lot more paperwork, insurance nonsense, and administrative drag than most people outside medicine realize.

Is your job stressful? How do you handle the stress and demands of your day?

Ha-ha, yes, of course it’s stressful. If you’re in cardiology and you’re not stressed sometimes, then you’re probably not taking the work seriously enough. But stress by itself is not the problem. The real issue is whether you let it control you. You deal with one problem, then the next one, and you stop wasting energy on panic. You’ll learn that too. Right now you still react to everything emotionally first. Later, if you stay in this field, you’ll learn how to pause, think, and move.

What is your greatest skill as a cardiologist?

I think one of my biggest strengths is that I know how to separate the job from my personal feelings. That takes time. *Dr. Reyes smiled.* You still do that. I could see it when you were shadowing me. You’re still looking at every patient like, “How do I fix their whole life?” And the truth is, you can’t. You can diagnose, treat, explain, recommend, and do your job well, but you can’t force people to take care of themselves or make better choices. That part is hard when you’re young, because you take it personally. That doesn’t mean you stop caring. It just means you learn how to care without letting it eat you alive.

What is your greatest weakness as a cardiologist?

I can be impatient, especially when things are preventable and the same mistakes keep repeating. People are acting childish all the time. Cardiology has a lot of that because so much of what we deal with is tied to long-term habits, follow-up, and adherence. You can give the right advice, the right medications, the right plan, and still watch people ignore all of it until the problem gets worse. *he shook his head a little* That’s annoying. The simplest example. Too many times we needed to reschedule a procedure because patients did not follow instructions from my anesthesiologists or nurses, or even mine. Simplest things they could do that are important for us.

What are the best and worst things about the place you work?

The best part is being able to take care of patients in San Antonio *previously when I was shadowing Dr. Reyes we talked about how San Antonio is a great city to be a cardiologist at because, on average, the population is obese and struggles with heart issues, and I noticed a lot of frequently returning patients* There’s value in seeing people over time and not just as random cases passing through. Jenice and Adolpho *Dr. Reyes’s assistant and nurse* are also very fun to work with, great team we built. The worst part is the same thing affecting almost every medical practice now like insurance barriers and documentation. Necessary, very important as for a nurse stuff, but very annoying.

What does a typical day at work look like for you?

It depends on whether that’s a procedure or surgery day or just an appointment day at the clinic. The clinic opens at 8 am, and it is usually a chill day. A lunch break for the clinic’s team from 12 to 1pm. Appointments, appointments, appointments. Usually, I spend the last hour of our clinic’s business hours doing documentation so the last appointment usually starts at 3:30 maybe 4 pm. After that I am going home. That’s it. Procedures or surgeries could start pretty early *the ones I observe started as early as 6am, if they were planned* and it is never something that you can predict. I am getting out earlier than anyone. Ha-ha, I always joke that I am working the most in the room, though.

What would your dream job be?

Honestly, this is pretty close to it. I wouldn’t say I dreamed about paperwork, insurance calls, and administrative nonsense, but the actual medicine, yes, this is the kind of work I wanted to do. If I could change anything, I would keep the patients, keep the cardiology, keep the decision-making, and remove half the bureaucracy. That would be the dream version of the job. Most doctors would probably tell you something similar.

If you had to start all over again, would you choose the same career? Why?

Yes, I probably would. But I would choose it with fewer illusions. Medicine is a good career, but it is not a gentle one. It takes a lot out of you. It demands your time, your attention, your energy, and sometimes more of your personal life than you planned to give. *he gave me half serious and half amused look* The question is not whether the career is worth it. The question is whether you are built for the reality.

My Reflection

The most unexpected thing I learned from this interview was how emotionally controlled you have to be to survive in cardiology long term. Before interviewing Dr. Reyes, I honestly thought I was holding myself together pretty well and acting professionally with patients. I did not think I looked overly emotional or too personally involved. But based on what he told me after, he saw it very differently. He said I was still reacting to patients too sympathetically and that after some visits I looked upset over things we could not actually change for them. That surprised me because in my head I thought I was being caring in the right way, but to him I was still acting like a student who takes too much home. His answers were more blunt and realistic than my first impression of the field. Before this, I saw cardiology as serious, intelligent, and high stakes, but I still viewed it in a more idealistic way. Dr. Reyes made it sound much more harsh and practical. He talked about how often patients ignore advice, make bad choices, or come back worse because they did not listen. He clearly thinks I have strong altruistic qualities, but he also made it obvious that in this field that can become a weakness if I do not learn how to manage it. He basically believes I am too gentle with some patients and too willing to believe their excuses or their version of events. My opinion of the field has not changed in a negative way, but it has become more realistic. I still find cardiology very interesting, but now I understand better that being good at it is not only about knowledge. It is also about boundaries, judgment, and emotional discipline. The main skills I need to improve are emotional control, clinical confidence, better separation between empathy and over-identification, and the ability to stay objective even when I feel bad for the patient.
